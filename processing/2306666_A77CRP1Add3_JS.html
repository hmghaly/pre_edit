Policy Brief 4 : ‘Valuing What Counts’ -
Framework to Progress Beyond GDP
Summary
The challenges we face can only be addressed through stronger international cooperation. The challenges that we face can only be addressed through stronger international cooperation. The Summit of the Future in 2024 is an opportunity to agree on multilateral solutions for a better tomorrow, by strengthening global governance for both present and future generations (General Assembly resolution 76/307). In my capacity as Secretary-General, I have been invited to provide inputs to the preparations for the Summit in the form of action-oriented recommendations, building on the proposals contained in my report entitled “Our Common Agenda” (A/75/982), which was itself a response to the declaration on the commemoration of the seventy-fifth anniversary of the United Nations (General Assembly resolution 75/1). The present policy brief is one such input. It elaborates on the ideas first proposed in Our Common Agenda, taking into account subsequent guidance from Member States and over one year of intergovernmental and multi-stakeholder consultations , and rooted in the purposes and the principles of the Charter of the United Nations, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and other international instruments.
Purpose of this policy brief
The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development and Our Common Agenda recognize a harmful anachronism at the heart of global policymaking, that our economic models and measurements overlook many aspects that sustain life and contribute to human well-being, while perversely placing disproportionate value on activities that deplete the planet. The intention of the proposals presented in this policy brief is not to replace Gross Domestic Product (GDP ) , but to outline a path to complementary metrics to GDP that more completely focus on what matters to people, the planet, and the future.
Today, the world is facing catastrophic and interconnected crises – including climate change, deteriorating ecosystems and biodiversity loss, devastating conflicts , and violence, increasing poverty, hunger, inequalities, unsustainable debt burdens, and heightened costs of living – which show no sign of abating. The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) are badly off-track. GDP has become a proxy for how we determine value, how we measure wealth creation and development progress , and how we allocate resources on that basis.
As mentioned in my Our Common Agenda report, GDP does not account for human well-being, environmental sustainability, unpaid household services, such as the care economy , and the unequal impact of economic activity. Moreover, it fails to capture the human and environmental destruction of some economic activities. Harmful practices, such as deforestation, overfishing, the burning of fossil fuels and other destruction of nature, often count as an increase in GDP. To achieve the targets we have set to address the triple planetary and other crises, a fundamental shift in how we measure progress is urgently needed.
Discussions on moving ‘Beyond GDP’ have been ongoing for decades.These efforts have laid the foundations for establishing a shared language and community of practice to go beyond GDP.
Examples of recent initiatives include:
The 2030 Agenda and the SDGs Sustainable Development Goals with their economic, social , and environmental dimensions.
Human development indices (UNDP), supplementing GDP with wellbeing well-being and agency, inequality, multidimensional poverty , and considering the interconnectedness of socioeconomic and planetary systems.
Gender-specific indicators across thematic areas that go beyond SDGs Sustainable Development Goals and adequately measure and compare the situation of women and men in all areas of life, including measures of time spent on unpaid care and domestic work and its distribution and economic value, along with other measures such as the economic cost of violence against women.
Inclusive growth and Beyond GDP work (Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD, European Commission, World Bank, IMF ) .
Measures of environmental degradation and depletion, environmental and economic accounting, and measures of inclusive or comprehensive wealth by the UN Environment Programme and World Bank.
Of these, the SDGs Sustainable Development Goals and their indicators, universally adopted by Member States, were consciously crafted to address the shortcomings of GDP. Indeed, the SDGs Sustainable Development Goals and their indicators are the most comprehensive ‘Beyond GDP’ framework agreed to and measured today. Moreover, SDG target 17.19 specifically calls for Member States to “build on existing initiatives to develop measurements of progress on sustainable development that complement Gross Domestic Product and support statistical capacity-building in developing countries” by 2030.
This call was taken up in Our Common Agenda, and the UN High-Level Committee on Programmes’ (HLCP) report ‘HYPERLINK "https://unsceb.org/valuing-what-counts-united-nations-system-wide-contribution-beyond-gross-domestic-product-gdp \hValuing What Counts: United Nations System Contribution on Progress Beyond Gross Domestic Product,’ which serves as the technical foundation to this policy brief. This brief also aims to build on ongoing processes, including the multi-year update of the United Nations System of National Accounts (SNA) by 2025 and the implementation of the System of Environmental-Economic Accounting (SEEA) and SEEA Ecosystem Accounting.
Building on that work, I now present proposals to help develop a universal and comprehensive measurement of progress and sustainable development to complement GDP. There are three concrete recommendations for Member States to consider:
a renewed political commitment to create a conceptual value framework that can accurately ‘value what counts’ for people, the planet, and the future, anchored in the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development and its commitment to leave no one behind;
the elaboration of a robust technical, scientific process, informed by sound and disaggregated data, resulting in a UN United Nations value dashboard of a limited number of key indicators of Beyond GDP; and
a major capacity - building and resourcing initiative to enable Member States to use the new framework effectively.
Why do we need to move Beyond GDP?
GDP is the most widely used benchmark to measure a country's economic progress and the value of its domestic production of goods and services. It is part of the SNA Somali National Army that sets out a common language for statisticians, economists, and policymakers across all countries. GDP can be considered a success story insofar as it has enabled thousands of national accountants worldwide to produce reliable, consistent, comparable, and replicable national accounts. And it provides a useful way to tell a compelling economic narrative.
It has, however, been used in unintended ways. By using GDP or income as a proxy for development, we fail to recognize that sustainable development is multidimensional and affected by multiple factors, such as access to resources, productivity traps, social and environmental dependencies, inequalities, vulnerabilities, challenges related to institutional capacity , and the significant vulnerabilities that may exist in countries with high GDP levels. Similar levels of national GDP can obscure the very different development realities, vulnerabilities and challenges that exist in different countries.
And yet, GDP continues to play a key role as a yardstick for providing international support, including in criteria for countries classified as least developed or eligible for support by the International Development Association, leaving some countries with high vulnerabilities without access to concessional financing. It oversimplifies and obscures the complex needs of countries in special circumstances – including middle-income countries, small island developing States and landlocked developing countries.
GDP emerged as a tool to address the crises of the 20th twentieth century but fails to sufficiently address the environmental and social concerns of the world today. For example:
GDP does not account for air pollution, natural resource depletion, environmental degradation and biodiversity loss. Rather, these negative externalities often drive increases in GDP without consideration of the broader, longer-term socioeconomic and environmental damage involved. Global GDP has doubled since 1970, while the depletion of resources more than tripled, with disastrous impacts on the natural environment. GDP contributes to the perception that permanent economic growth without reference to environmental and social considerations can reasonably continue indefinitely within planetary boundaries.
GDP does not capture the full extent of the informal economy and the social value of activities, such as unpaid care work in households, nor does it capture the social value of activities, such as health, the value of security, or that of free goods delivered online. For example, unpaid care work done largely by women is often invisible and undervalued. This was particularly pronounced during the COVID-19 pandemic, when women spent an estimated 512 billion hours globally doing additional unpaid childcare work that sustained our economies.
GDP does not offer a lens on distributions to identify and address inequalities, including between women and men, refugees, migrants, minorities, nor does it account for the targeted interventions needed to address the needs of people facing intersectional forms of inequality. GDP's social and environmental exclusion perpetuates the misallocation of resources in ways that further exacerbate inequalities.
Due to serious compilation and measurement challenges GDP still often insufficiently captures new economic phenomena, for instance digitalization and the use of free digital services, the use of crypto assets cryptoassets and the development of data. These particular issues are being addressed in the current discussion of the 2025 revision of the SNA Somali National Army .
Over time, it has become clear that there are intrinsic limitations on what GDP can measure. It does not fully capture the complexities of the urgent challenges we face today. Despite these evident shortcomings, GDP continues to be used - or misused - as the benchmark for important national and international policy settings, particularly in particular in respect of development financing. This notably includes criteria of eligibility for Official Development Assistance (ODA) and decisions on debt relief and concessional financing, as well as matters such as graduation from Least Developed Country (LDC) status. This excessive dependence on GDP has created damaging bottlenecks and grave inequities in the functioning of the international financial architecture with serious consequences for the sustainable development of all countries , and for Middle-Income Countries and Small Island Developing States, in particular.
These problems need to be addressed urgently, including through the application of the Beyond GDP metrics that I propose. The use of metrics in the context of financial decision - making will be dealt with more fully in the forthcoming Policy Brief on the International Financial Architecture. This will also need to build upon the forthcoming recommendations of the High-Level Panel on the Development of a Multidimensional Vulnerability Index (MVI), which can also contribute importantly to correcting the longstanding long-standing inequitable treatment resulting from the uncritical use of GDP as a predominant policy benchmark.
Going Beyond GDP, to accurately measure what is valuable, and to enhance decision-making in the best interests of people, the planet, and the future, can fundamentally change the way governments make policies and spur investments so as to accelerate achievement of the SDGs and leave no one behind. There are a number of opportunities ahead of us to identify and develop Beyond GDP metrics. The SNA Somali National Army , in which GDP is embedded, was revised in 1968, 1993, and 2008, and is currently being revised again with a view to completion by 2025. The effort to go beyond GDP should capitalize on the opportunity of this update to the SNA Somali National Army , which will be focused on the measurement of digitalization and globalization, to better account for wellbeing well-being and sustainability, including distributional aspects, the informal economy , and unpaid household service work.
In addition, many countries are developing statistics for the System of Environmental-Economic Accounting to analyze analyse and measure not only how the economy impacts the environment, but also how ecosystems function and contribute directly to our well-being. Current socio-economic socioeconomic statistics , and data from population and housing censuses, compiled based on international statistical standards, should also be part of the foundations to go beyond GDP.
In addition to compiling headline indicators, Member States will need to maintain and develop comprehensive national data ecosystems to analyse and provide disaggregated data to assess the achievement of the principle of leaving no one behind. Indeed, today's multiple crises, including the escalating climate emergency, have revealed that our statistical systems need to move away from current, traditional ways of operating to be able to effectively support countries’ sustainable development and transformation agendas.

A Framework to Value What Counts
Moving beyond GDP is fundamental to building an economic system that gives value to what counts – human wellbeing now, in the future and for everyone. Effectively valuing what counts can have a transformative impact by better informing decision-making for a more just, inclusive , and sustainable development path.
To this end, I present three proposals.
A Strong Political Commitment
First, I propose that Member States make an explicit commitment to go Beyond GDP by agreeing a conceptual value framework, firmly anchored in the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development , by the time of the Summit of the Future in 2024. The HLCP report provides a basis for this conceptual framework. That report draws on research carried out across the UN United Nations system and beyond, including the discussions on GDP – its strength, limitations and uses – and the identification of issues at stake to formulate a framework for policy and metrics.
This framework to ‘Value What Counts’ should be designed to achieve three main outcomes
Well-being and agency: putting the focus on people and promote meaningful participation to ensure decisions reflect people's needs and enable everyone's contribution to transformational change;
Respect for life and the planet: safeguarding the planet and ensure possibilities for life and well-being in the future; and
Reduced inequalities and greater solidarity: leading to more equal distribution of well-being.
The framework to ‘Value What Counts’ should also consider a path of transformation through three additional elements:
Participatory governance and stronger institutions: steering us towards outcomes ensuring equitable, inclusive , and safe societal conditions empowering everyone to participate, contribute, and benefit safely and effectively (social dimension);
Innovative and ethical economies: serving people and the planet through innovation to find collective solutions to our challenges, with responsible and ethical actions to deliver positive outcomes that uphold people's rights (economic dimension);
From vulnerability to resilience: focusing on our interaction with the natural and built environment to strengthen preparedness and ensure well-being given multiple risks and uncertainties (environmental dimension).
A Robust Technical Scientific Process
Second, the political commitment to develop a conceptual framework to ‘Value What Counts’ must go hand in hand with a technical scientific process to develop the metrics that inform this framework. In this regard, I propose the establishment of a High-Level Expert Group of independent experts with a mandate to produce an initial value dashboard of a limited number of key indicators (ideally not more than 10-20 indicators) of Beyond GDP by March 2024. This should be presented for Member States’ consideration in preparation for the Summit of the Future.
This High-Level Expert Group should be multidisciplinary, including national and international experts with a spectrum of policy-makers, statisticians, civil society , and academia. The technical work of selecting and verifying data indicators should be undertaken through the UN Statistical Commission, that also hosts the SDG indicator framework, to ensure robust and scientific accuracy of the metrics.
One of the strengths of GDP is its conciseness. While GDP is anything but simple, it manages to summarize information in an intuitive manner that tells a story. The framework to ‘Value What Counts’ must retain these strengths. It must be concise, widely accepted, comparable, and applicable to decision-making.
At the same time, well-being, equality , and environmental sustainability are complex multidimensional phenomena that cannot be addressed by a single summary indicator, like GDP. This warrants the development of a broader measurement framework to monitor and analyse the multiple aspects of progress and enable a better understanding of trade-offs and consequences.
Therefore, the aim should not be to establish a single composite indicator of ‘Beyond GDP’. An indicator of everything would summarize too much and reveal too little to be able to adequately inform policy. Instead, I propose the selection of a set of core metrics that would be assessed, developed and selected through a scientific, multidisciplinary process , and which would need to be:
comparable across time and countries, well-established and trusted;
country owned;
universally applicable;
able to convey strong and clear messages that are actionable and intuitive;
scientifically robust and statistically sound; and
iterative and dynamic based on what exists, while adding new indicators, as relevant.
The methodology and process to develop these indicators should build upon current capacities and ongoing work and incorporate appropriately the results of the High-Level Panel on the Development of a Multidimensional Vulnerability Index (MVI), as well as existing indices and indicators including the SDG Sustainable Development Goal indicators, the Human Development Index and other relevant indicators which consider human rights and gender equality.
A Major Capacity - Building Initiative
Third, I propose a major strengthening of UN United Nations support to countries in stepping up statistical capacity development and enabling country-owned use and reporting of progress beyond GDP. Such national capacity development will also respond to the global challenge to develop and propose new metrics to complement GDP in a participatory process and to fill persisting gaps in SDG reporting. GDP succeeded in becoming universally recognized and the main benchmark to measure a country's economic progress and the value of its domestic production of goods and services because it was accompanied by massive investments and support to develop statistical capacity at the national level. Going Beyond GDP will require comparably ambitious international investment in capacity development if it is to be successful.
The UN System United Nations system must support statistical systems to shift priorities towards new metrics, including more comprehensive accounting of stocks and flows, distributions, sustainability and intergenerational perspectives, vulnerabilities , and aspects related to innovation, governance, stability, participation , and human rights.
In addition to a dashboard, Member States will need to be empowered to have comprehensive datasets that enable disaggregation. As metrics will need to develop beyond today's needs, they cannot be limited by the data we have today. Building strong and dynamic statistical ecosystems and strong statistical capacity will also be key to enabling agility to measure what counts as and when new challenges arise.
With the support of adequate capacity - building, the dashboard will eventually form a high-quality, widely available, and comparable body of information based on global and national official statistics , and new sources and technologies.
The pandemic challenged statistical offices and generated innovation, affecting the way in which they collect data from multiple sources, including big data and geospatial data , and how they share and compile data to provide timely insights. Many statistical offices serve as national ‘data stewards’ ensuring that data is used as an asset to society. New capacity development efforts should ensure innovation and that the use of new technologies is accessible to all countries.
Valuing What Counts: Recommendations for Member States
Action to go Beyond GDP must be driven by a clear and shared political commitment and underpinned by strengthened technical capacity and expertise.
I urge Member States to:
Confirm their political commitment to develop a conceptual value framework on Beyond GDP anchored in the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development ,
This framework should be designed to achieve the following outcomes:
Wellbeing and agency
Respect for life and the planet
Reduced inequalities and greater solidarity
It should be based on the following elements to achieve a transformative process of change:
Participatory governance and stronger institutions
Innovative and ethical economies
From vulnerability to resilience
Establish an independent High-level Expert Group to elaborate a value dashboard of a limited number of key indicators of Beyond GDP beyond gross domestic product (not more than 10-20), taking the HLCP report, ‘Valuing What Counts: United Nations System Contribution on Progress Beyond Gross Domestic Product’, as a starting point, and to present the outcomes by March 2024 for Member States’ consideration in preparation for the Summit of the Future
This High-Level Expert Group should:
be multidisciplinary and include experts across the spectrum of policy makers, statisticians, civil society and academia.
Ensure the methodology and selection of indicators builds upon current capacities, the ongoing work to develop the Multidimensional Vulnerability Index (MVI), and existing indices and indicators, including the SDG Sustainable Development Goal indicators, the Human Development Index and relevant thematic indicators which consider human rights and gender equality.
Resource and strengthen statistical capacity-building and data collection, in particular at the national-level, to support Beyond GDP beyond gross domestic product , as well as filling gaps in SDG reporting. The going Beyond GDP process will require significant investments in data and statistical capacities, particularly in particular at the national-level , and in developing countries. In addition to compiling headline indicators, Member States will need to maintain and develop comprehensive national data ecosystems to analyse distributions, disaggregation, and the achievement of the principle of leaving no one behind.
Conclusion
Discussions on going Beyond GDP have been ongoing for decades. Now is the time for action. Primarily relying on GDP as a metric of progress does not accurately reflect the world as it is – nor the world that we want future generations to inhabit. We need a paradigm shift in what we measure as progress so that we can capture data on the activities and outcomes that a society truly values – and then use them to better inform our policy and financial decisions. We have an opportunity to shape our future into one that is more equal and resilient to crises, and where the benefits of social and economic progress are shared by all. By moving Beyond GDP beyond gross domestic product , we can measure what we truly value, what we mean by progress, and on that basis, how we distribute resources and commit to real change.
